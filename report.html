Overview

Under traditional fee-for-service reimbursement, a primary care practice has limited financial incentive to reduce hospitalizations among their patient population. Value-based care is a healthcare delivery and payment models that reward providers for the quality of care and cost efficient. For example, a primary care practice might enter a financial contract with an insurance company that involves reimbursing the practice for limiting the total healthcare expenditures for patients attributed to that practice.

Hospital admissions are a substantial component of total US healthcare spending. Reducing hospital readmissions during the 30-day post-discharge window is an opportunity to improve patient outcomes while reducing costs. Under fee-for-service, there is little incentive to invest in post-discharge care management to reduce readmission rates. However, value-based care models offer an opportunity for primary care providers to receive reimbursement for the savings obtained by reducing readmissions.

The Research Question

For a program designed to reduce the prevalence of hospital readmissions, it is important to assess the impact of that program in order to determine if the cost of the program justifies the reduction in healthcare spend and improvement in patient outcomes.

In this analysis, we use simulated data to demonstrate estimtaing the impact of a primary care practice incorporating post-discharge care management on the probability of hospital readmission for any cause during 30 days.

Drivers of Readmission

There are many factors beyond care management which may impact the probability of readmission, including:

  • patient clinical factors such as primary diagnosis and comorbidities
  • social drivers of heath including access to housing, social support, and transportation
  • quality of primary care

Simulated Data

For this analysis, we simulate data on practices, patients, and hospitalizations.

In a real-world scenario, we would expect to see the following in the data:

  • independent practices will have varying numbers of providers
  • the number of patients per practice will vary based on the number of providers and the payer max (e.g. heavy medicare practices tend to have smaller patient panels)
  • the timing of the intervention will vary across practices
  • the rate of both hospitalization and readmission will vary by both patient and practice characteristics

The simulated data for this analysis (available [here]) incorporates all of these features.

Compute Patient Outcome

Unfold this ‘Code’ to see the code for joining the three tables together and computing the binary treatment indicator for care-management.

Code 
patient_outcome <- pat %>%
  left_join(prac, by = "practice_id") %>%
  left_join(hosp, by = c("practice_id", "patient_id")) %>%
  mutate(
    has_intervention_pt = case_when(
      has_intervention == 0 ~ 0,
      # if the patient is hospitalized before the practice adopts care managment,
      # the patient has a 0 for treatment even if the practice subsequently adopts care management
      intervention_start > hosp_date ~ 0,
      TRUE ~ 1
    ),
    hosp_date_days = as.integer(hosp_date - as.Date("2022-01-01")),
  )

Model

Inclusion Criteria

  • any patient hospitalized since 2023-01-01
  • patient does not have ESRD diagnosis

Model Consideration

  • should allow for baseline decreasing rate of readmissions over time
  • should allow for practice and patient level covariates
  • should allow for variation among practices beyond what is captured in practice-level covariates

GLMER Model

  • random effect induces a compound symmetry structure on the patient-level correlation matrix

\[ P(Y_{ij} = 1) = logit^{-1}(\alpha_j + \beta T_i + \gamma'X_i) \]

\[ \alpha_j \sim N(0, \sigma^2_j) \]

Code 
m2 <- glmer(
  readmit ~ (1 | practice_id) + has_intervention_pt + hosp_date_days + age + sex + ssdi,
  family = binomial,
  data = model_data
)

# other options: profile, boot
m2_confint <- confint(m2, method = "Wald") %>%
    as_tibble(rownames = "covariate") %>%
    rename(
        lower95 = `2.5 %`,
        upper95 = `97.5 %`
    )
    
m2_coeff <- summary(m2)$coefficients %>%
    as_tibble(rownames = "covariate") %>%
    rename(se = `Std. Error`)

m2_coeff %>% inner_join(m2_confint, by = "covariate") %>%
    select(covariate, Estimate, se, lower95, upper95) %>%
    mutate(
        est_odds = exp(Estimate),
        lower95_odds = exp(lower95),
        upper95_odds = exp(upper95)
    ) %>%
    select(covariate, est_odds, lower95_odds, upper95_odds) %>%
    gt() %>%
    fmt_number(c(est_odds, lower95_odds, upper95_odds), decimals = 3)
